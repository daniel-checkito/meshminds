The Sellability Formula
Why scoring beats guessing
The big idea

Most sellers pick products based on what they enjoy printing. That's backwards. The market doesn't care what you enjoy — it buys what it needs or desires. The gap between a great print and a selling print is almost always demand, not quality.

MeshMinds scores every product on five axes: market demand, competition, profit margin, legal risk, and manufacturing fit. A product that scores well on all five is a winner. Most products fail on one or two.

The formula

Sellability = Demand × Differentiation ÷ Competition

High demand alone isn't enough — if 80,000 sellers already offer the same thing (generic keychains, flexi dragons), the market is saturated. The real money is in niches where demand is clear and competition is still fragmented.

Rule #1: Before printing a single gram, check how many Etsy listings exist for that product. Under 5,000? Worth exploring. Over 50,000 with no obvious differentiation angle? Walk away.

Reading Demand Signals
Finding products buyers actually want
Where demand comes from

On Etsy, demand = search volume. The number of people typing a keyword per month is the closest thing to a demand forecast you'll get. MeshMinds estimates this for every product it scores.

But search volume alone isn't demand — it's attention. Real demand shows up as sales velocity: how many units are the top 5 listings moving per month? Check the "X sales" badge under Etsy listings. If the top 5 sellers combined are doing fewer than 100 sales/month across a niche, the market is shallow.

Green vs red demand signals
  • Keyword has 2,000–20,000 monthly searches on Etsy
  • Top listings have 500+ reviews (proves real transactions)
  • Gift or occasion-driven (birthday, wedding, pet) — evergreen cycle
  • Product solves a specific, named problem ("cable tidy for standing desk")
  • Passionate niche: DnD players, keyboard enthusiasts, plant parents
  • Keyword broad and generic ("phone stand", "keychain")
  • Trend-driven with no sustained search history
  • No Etsy listings have more than 50 reviews — market hasn't proven out
  • Product is cool but solves no clear problem or emotional need
Shortcut: Search your keyword on Etsy, sort by "Most relevant", and count how many of the top 20 listings have over 100 reviews. If fewer than 5 do, the product is unproven.

Beating the Competition
When to enter a crowded niche — and when not to
The listing count trap

New sellers see 40,000 Etsy listings for "desk organiser" and think the niche is dead. Sometimes they're right. But listing count alone is a bad signal — what matters is whether those listings are visually differentiated.

If you search a category and the first 20 results look nearly identical (same shape, same colours, same photos), that category is saturated. But if you see 20 different aesthetic takes — minimalist, maximalist, character-themed, wooden-look — that's a creative niche with room for one more distinctive entry.

How to enter a competitive niche
  • Specialise the keyword: "desk organiser" → "mushroom desk organiser cottagecore" (800 listings, not 40,000)
  • Add personalisation: Any product with a name slot is automatically differentiated from generic versions
  • Reposition as décor: A "cable tray" becomes a "minimal cable management piece" at twice the price
  • Target a sub-audience: "cat lover desk set" appeals to a buyer who won't even look at a generic organiser

Pricing for Profit
The true cost of a print and how to price above it
The real cost formula

Most sellers only count filament. That's the fastest route to working below minimum wage.

True cost per unit = filament + electricity + failure rate (6–8%) + packaging + Etsy fees (~10%) + your time.

At €0.025/g PLA, a 50g print costs €1.25 in filament. Add packaging (€0.40), failure overhead (€0.11), Etsy fee on a €15 listing (€1.50 + €0.20), and even at minimum wage (€12/hr) for 20 minutes of handling — that's €4 per unit before profit. On a €15 sale, you're making ~€9. That's fine — but only if your print time is under 2 hours. A 6-hour print at €0.025/g costs €3.50 in filament, but the opportunity cost of tying up a printer for 6 hours at 2–3 units/day caps your volume badly.

Price anchoring tactics
  • Always have a bundle (2-pack, 3-pack) — it raises AOV and reduces per-unit shipping cost
  • Offer personalisation as a premium add-on (+€5–8 per item) — it costs you 2 minutes to change a name in the slicer
  • Run a permanent 20% "sale" — Etsy notifies watchers, and buyers feel they're getting a deal
  • Anchor high: list at €29.99, then discount to €22. The original price becomes the perceived value.
Gross margin target: Aim for at least 60% gross margin (filament + packaging only). Below 50% and you have no room for promotions, returns, or price pressure from competitors.

Photography That Converts
Why your listing photos are your #1 sales tool
The thumbnail test

On Etsy, buyers see your thumbnail at roughly 200×200px against 19 competitors. You have about 0.3 seconds to make them click. The entire sale lives or dies in that moment.

The #1 mistake: photographing your print against a plain white background. It looks like a prototype, not a product. The best-selling listings show the item in context — on a real desk, in a real room, held by real hands. This is called lifestyle photography and it's the single biggest lever for conversion.

7-shot listing formula
  • Shot 1 (hero): Lifestyle — item in use, styled environment, natural light
  • Shot 2: Close-up showing surface texture and print quality
  • Shot 3: Scale reference — item next to a hand, coin, or known object
  • Shot 4: Flat lay with complementary props
  • Shot 5: All colour/finish variants together
  • Shot 6: Packaging photo — builds trust
  • Shot 7: Infographic — dimensions, materials, care instructions
No DSLR needed: A modern smartphone + a window with indirect daylight + a €15 piece of white foam board as a reflector will beat 90% of 3D print listings on Etsy today. The bar is shockingly low.

Legal Landmines
IP, licences and what Etsy actually enforces
The two legal risks

1. Intellectual property (IP): Disney, Nintendo, Marvel, Pokémon, Harry Potter, and hundreds of other brands actively scan Etsy and file DMCA takedowns. An infringing listing gets pulled within days, and repeat violations lead to permanent shop suspension. The risk is real and well-documented — don't trade short-term sales for a banned account.

2. Licence compliance: Selling prints from a file you downloaded requires a commercial licence from the designer. Most files on MakerWorld, Printables, and Cults3D are licensed for personal use only. Check the licence tab on every file page before printing to sell. When in doubt, message the designer — many grant commercial licences for free or a small fee.

Safe vs risky categories
  • Original designs you created yourself — full commercial freedom
  • Files with explicit "commercial use allowed" or CC-BY licence
  • Generic functional items (stands, holders, organizers) — no IP angle
  • Character-adjacent but original designs (a cat planter that isn't Garfield)
  • Any file depicting Disney, Nintendo, Marvel, DC, or major game characters
  • Logos, brand names, or slogans printed onto items
  • Files marked "personal use only" or "non-commercial"
  • Fan art of living celebrities without their permission
Practical rule: If a child would recognise it as a character from a movie or game — don't sell it on Etsy. You can often sell the same design on your own website or at craft fairs with lower enforcement risk, but Etsy will pull the listing.

Finding Your Next Winner
A repeatable system for product discovery
The product discovery loop

Successful 3D print sellers don't stumble onto winning products — they run a repeatable discovery process every week. Here's the one that works:

  • Browse MakerWorld "Popular this week": High download count = real interest. Cross-reference with Etsy to see if prints are being sold
  • Mine Etsy search autocomplete: Type a category ("planter", "cable", "stand") and read the autocomplete suggestions — each suggestion is a real search term with real volume
  • Reddit product communities: r/malelivingspace, r/battlestations, r/homelab, r/houseplants — people share what they wish they could buy. That's unsatisfied demand
  • Pinterest trends: Interior design trends arrive on Pinterest 6–12 months before they peak on Etsy. Cottagecore planters, office cable management, bathroom aesthetics — all peaked on Pinterest first
  • Score everything with MeshMinds: Paste any model URL or describe your idea to get an objective score before investing print time
The 5-product test rule

Never build a full shop around one product until you've validated it. The pro approach: list 5 different products simultaneously, invest equal effort in each (good photos, optimised titles), and observe which gets views and favourites within the first 2 weeks. Double down on the winner, pause the rest. The market tells you what it wants — listen to it before scaling.
